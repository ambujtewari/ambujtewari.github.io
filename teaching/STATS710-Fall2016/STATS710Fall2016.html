  • Description: Mobile health offers us an unprecedented opportunity to help people with long-term health behavior change. Smartphones and wearable devices have remarkable sensing capabilities allowing us to understand the context in which a person is at a given moment. These devices also have the ability to deliver interventions tailored to the specific needs of users in a given location at a given time. Figuring out which interventions lead to improved health outcomes for which people in what contexts is a fundamental decision making problem in mobile health. This advanced level graduate course will focus on theory and algorithms for sequential decision making algorithms with an emphasis on mobile health applications.
    The course will cover the following topics: (1) multi-armed bandit problems, (2) contextual bandit problems, (3) reinforcement learning in MDPs (Markov Decision Processes), and (4) special topics of interest in mobile health applications. Subtopics in the last category might include transfer learning (using data on other similar people to enable faster learning), non-stationarity (dealing with slowly changing or abrupt changes in user behavior), interpretability of policies (enabling communication with behavioral scientists by making connections to behavioral theories), computation/communication tradeoffs (one-the-phone vs in-the-cloud computations), myopic/long-term planning tradeoffs (myopic approaches can be more lightweight, both computationally and with regards to theoretical analyses, but ignore impact of our interventions on future user contexts), and user engagement (a large fractions of health apps are abandoned after a small number of uses).

Grading

This is an advanced graduate level course. Our expectation is that you will engage in the course out of intellectual curiosity. The formal course requirements will be rather minimal. The final grade in the course will be determined by the quality of your scribed lecture notes and one final project using the weights given below.

  • Scribing Lectures (60%): Each of you will scribe 1-2 lectures. This means taking notes during class and typing them up in LaTeX. The scribed notes will be posted on the course webpage.
  • Sign up for scribing lecture notes via Canvas->Collaborations->Scribing Schedule
  • Download the template, useful macros, and the bib file.
  • Modify the template and fill it with your notes.
  • If you make additions to the macros or bib file, make sure to do that at the very end.
  • For bibtex entries, try using Google Scholar. Search for the paper you want to cite, click on “Cite” and then click on “Bibtex” in the window that opens.
  • Email me your notes with name lecXX.tex (where XX is the lecture number) along with macros and bib files in case you made additions to those.
  • Look at tex files from other lecture notes and try to keep your notation and style consistent with earlier ones.
  • Final Project (40%): An important component of the course is a final project which can either be:
  • a survey of some actively developing sub-topic within sequential decision making, or
  • a detailed commentary on a classic paper on sequential decision making, or
  • a research project involving contributing novel research (theoretical result, statistical method, computational algorithm) to the area of sequential decision making
  • Project credit breakup: Surveys have to be written individually. However, teams of up to 2 students can be formed for a research project. To get full credit, surveys have to be very high quality: they should be similar to a publishable survey article in a top journal. The bar for research projects will be lower because of the time constraint and the inherent uncertainty in the research process. While you’re not required to deliver publication quality research work by the end of the semester, you’re encouraged to do so. We will provide some suggestions for research projects but you should feel free to work on any problem that interests you. The 40% credit for the project will be split as follows:
  • Project proposal (5%)
  • Project poster presentation in class (10%)
  • Project final report (25%)

Topics

A tentative list of topics is as follows.

  • Multi-armed Bandits: regret analysis, major algorithm families (phased exploration with greedy exploitation, upper confidence bounds, posterior sampling aka Thompson sampling), adversarial setting (Exp3 algorithm)
  • Contextual Bandits: regret analysis in three settings (stochastic contexts & rewards, adversarial context & stochastic rewards, adversarial contexts and rewards), parametric (LinUCB and Thompson Sampling with Gaussian linear model) and nonparametric modeling of reward functions, competing with policies (Exp4, epoch greedy, RUCB, ILOVETOCONBANDITS)
  • Learning in MDPs: Basics of MDPs (value functions, Bellman equations, value iteration, policy iteration, need for function approximation in real world problems), theoretical analysis of learning in ``small” MDPs: PAC-MDP literature (Rmax, MBIE, Delayed Q-learning, MorMax) and regret bounds (UCRL2), fundamental learning algorithms for practical problems ([gradient] temporal difference methods, least squares methods, policy gradient methods, actor-critic methods)
  • Challenges in Mobile Health: costly to acquire features (EMAs), non-stationarity, good initialization of policies, interpretability of policies, computational considerations on the phone, missing data, user engagement
